Background of the Study

Plastic pollution has become a global environmental and economic challenge, driven by the widespread use of single-use plastic products. In everyday life, individuals consume items like soda in plastic bags, chocolate bars wrapped in plastic, or food served on styrofoam plates. These materials, particularly polystyrene and polyethylene, are persistent pollutants that are difficult to recycle, especially in countries with limited disposal methods. Among these materials, polystyrene poses a significant problem, taking up to 500 years or more to degrade and contributing to landfill waste (Kelly, 2023; Davis, 2019). Efforts to mitigate this waste have included chemical methods, but researchers are increasingly exploring biological alternatives.

Among these materials, polystyrene, commonly known as styrofoam, poses a significant problem. Studies suggest that polystyrene can take up to 500 years or more to degrade, contributing to the long-term accumulation of waste in landfills (Kelly, 2023; Davis, 2019). Although styrofoam is lightweight, it occupies considerable space due to its low density—approximately 31% the density of cork—and makes up as much as 30% of some landfills (Davis, 2019). Worse, much of this plastic waste ends up polluting natural environments such as rivers, forests, and fields, further exacerbating environmental degradation.

Efforts to mitigate polystyrene waste have included chemical methods like using acetone to dissolve and compact the material. However, acetone is highly flammable and poses significant environmental and safety risks, making it an unsuitable solution for widespread use (Helmenstine, 2019 ; Spangler, 2024). This has led researchers to explore biological alternatives for plastic degradation, with a particular focus on organisms capable of consuming plastic materials.

One promising solution involves the use of superworms (Zophobas morio), which have demonstrated an ability to consume polystyrene and low-density polyethylene (LDPE). However, previous studies have been limited in scope, focusing primarily on polystyrene.

To address these gaps, this study aims to conduct a comparative analysis of the effectiveness of Zophobas morio in consuming both polystyrene and polyethylene over a 60-day period.

Significance of the Study

This study is significant for several reasons. Firstly, it addresses the pressing issue of plastic pollution, which has severe environmental and economic consequences. By exploring the potential of superworms in consuming plastics, this research contributes to the development of sustainable solutions for plastic waste management. Secondly, the study’s focus on a comparative analysis of polystyrene and polyethylene consumption by Zophobas morio provides valuable insights into the worms’ capabilities and limitations. This information can inform the development of more effective plastic degradation strategies. Finally, the study’s emphasis on the long-term ecological impact and feasibility of using superworms on an industrial scale highlights the need for further research in this area.

Scope and Limitations of the Study

This scope of the study is to compare the mass reduction of two types of plastics, polystyrene and polyethylene, when exposed to superworms (Zophobas morio) over a period of 60 days. The experiment will focus on these two plastics, with mass reduction as the primary indicator of degradation, aiming to assess whether superworms are more effective at reducing the mass of one plastic type over the other. However, the study is limited by its 60-day duration, which may not fully capture long-term degradation effects. Additionally, one mass reduction will be measured, without examining the chemical breakdown or byproducts, limiting insights into complete degradation. Experimental conditions may not replicate natural settings, potentially affecting the superworms’ consumption rates. Lastly, findings will be specific to polystyrene and polyethylene, limiting generalizability to other plastic types.

Problem Statement and Objectives

Problem Statement

The purpose of this study is to determine the effect of superworms (Zophobas morio) on the mass reduction of polystyrene and polyethylene over a 60-day period.

Objectives

This study aims to:

  1. To investigate the impact of Zophobas morio on the consumption of polyethylene and polystyrene.
  2. To compare the effectiveness of superworms in consuming polystyrene versus polyethylene by analyzing the difference in mass reduction after 60 days.
  3. To assess the potential for superworms to be used in practical applications for plastic waste management.

Hypothesis

If superworms (Zophobas morio) are exposed to polystyrene and polyethylene for 60 days, then the mass of both plastics will be reduced, with polystyrene experiencing a greater reduction in mass than polyethylene.
