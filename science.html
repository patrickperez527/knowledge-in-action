Background of the Study

Plastic pollution has become a global environmental and economic challenge, driven by the widespread use of single-use plastic products. In everyday life, individuals consume items like soda in plastic bags, chocolate bars wrapped in plastic, or food served on styrofoam plates. These materials, particularly polystyrene and polyethylene, are persistent pollutants that are difficult to recycle, especially in countries with limited disposal methods. Among these materials, polystyrene poses a significant problem, taking up to 500 years or more to degrade and contributing to landfill waste (Kelly, 2023; Davis, 2019). Efforts to mitigate this waste have included chemical methods, but researchers are increasingly exploring biological alternatives.

Among these materials, polystyrene, commonly known as styrofoam, poses a significant problem. Studies suggest that polystyrene can take up to 500 years or more to degrade, contributing to the long-term accumulation of waste in landfills (Kelly, 2023; Davis, 2019). Although styrofoam is lightweight, it occupies considerable space due to its low density—approximately 31% the density of cork—and makes up as much as 30% of some landfills (Davis, 2019). Worse, much of this plastic waste ends up polluting natural environments such as rivers, forests, and fields, further exacerbating environmental degradation.

Efforts to mitigate polystyrene waste have included chemical methods like using acetone to dissolve and compact the material. However, acetone is highly flammable and poses significant environmental and safety risks, making it an unsuitable solution for widespread use (Helmenstine, 2019 ; Spangler, 2024). This has led researchers to explore biological alternatives for plastic degradation, with a particular focus on organisms capable of consuming plastic materials.

One promising solution involves the use of superworms (Zophobas morio), which have demonstrated an ability to consume polystyrene and low-density polyethylene (LDPE). However, previous studies have been limited in scope, focusing primarily on polystyrene.

To address these gaps, this study aims to conduct a comparative analysis of the effectiveness of Zophobas morio in consuming both polystyrene and polyethylene over a 60-day period.
